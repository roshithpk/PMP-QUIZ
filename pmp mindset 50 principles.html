📌 Stakeholder Management — Principles 1–5
01 Continuously identify & analyze stakeholders — not just at initiation Stakeholder ▾

Stakeholders come and go throughout the project. A new CMO, a new regulator, a replaced team member — all are new stakeholders requiring fresh analysis.

The "Identify Stakeholders" process is listed in Initiating but is performed throughout the entire project.

⚠ TRAP: Don't assume stakeholder register is locked after initiation. Always update it when new stakeholders appear.
✅ ACTION: Meet with new stakeholder → understand concerns → update stakeholder register → update communication & engagement plan.
02 Engage stakeholders regularly via various channels Stakeholder ▾

Different stakeholders need different frequency and channel. Team = daily, Sponsor = weekly, CEO = monthly. One method for all is always wrong.

⚠ TRAP: "Increase email frequency" or "mandatory meeting for all" = wrong. One-size-fits-all is a red flag answer.
✅ KEY: Multi-channel approach tailored per stakeholder preference = correct answer.
03 Use Emotional Intelligence (EI) — address issues with that person privately Stakeholder ▾

EI = understanding your own emotions, their impact on others, and reading others' emotions. Detect when someone is unhappy even if they say "fine."

If one person is causing a problem — dominating meetings, demoralizing others — do NOT address them in front of everyone. Meet privately, one-on-one.

⚠ TRAP: Sending a group email about one person's issue = wrong. Group confrontation = wrong.
✅ ACTION: Detect emotional signal → reach out privately → understand root cause → then resolve.
04 Document ALL impacted individuals as stakeholders — including indirect & negatively impacted Stakeholder ▾

Anyone positively OR negatively impacted, directly OR indirectly = stakeholder. Bob losing his job due to your AI system is a stakeholder too.

⚠ TRAP: "They don't directly use the system so don't add them" = wrong.
✅ KEY: Add to stakeholder register → analyze needs → tailor communications.
05 Never dismiss customer requests — evaluate every one carefully Stakeholder ▾

We do projects FOR customers. Never push them off, ignore them, or just tell them "no." At minimum, assess and give feedback.

What to do FIRST when a client requests a scope change: Meet with them and understand the business value — BEFORE sending to change control or estimating with the team.

⚠ TRAP: "Tell them it's already approved and can't be changed" = dismissal = wrong answer.
✅ SEQUENCE: Meet → understand business value → then assess → then formal change process.
🔄 Change Management — Principles 6–8
06 Traditional: Follow the plan. Agile: Changes go to Product Owner via backlog Change ▾
Traditional Agile

Traditional: If it's not in the plan, don't do it. All changes require a formal change request and approval (sponsor / CCB).

Agile: Changes are welcome. They go to the Product Owner who decides whether to add to backlog and prioritizes accordingly. NO formal change request in agile.

⚠ TRAP: "Implement the change to keep project on track" = never the answer (traditional). "Reject the request" = never the answer (agile or traditional — it's not your authority).
07 Any stakeholder requesting changes to the PM plan must submit a change request (Traditional) Change ▾
Traditional

Even if the sponsor says "this needs to happen now" — ask for the formal change request first. Document it before assessing or acting.

⚠ TRAP: "Begin working immediately since the sponsor requested it" = implementing without a change request = wrong.
08 All changes must be reviewed and assessed across ALL knowledge areas before approval Change ▾
Traditional

A scope change impacts time, cost, quality, resources, communications, risk, procurement, stakeholders. Assess ALL of them — even "easy" 2-day changes.

⚠ TRAP: "Approve it since it's low risk / only 2 days" = PM doesn't have authority + no assessment = wrong.
✅ KEY: Conduct thorough assessment across all areas → THEN recommend for approval.
🧩 Decision-Making & Problem Solving — Principles 9–17
09 Never act without a plan — check your management plans before taking action Decision ▾
TraditionalAgile (sprint planning)

When issues arise, your management plans tell you how to respond. Schedule issue → Schedule Management Plan. Resource conflict → Resource Management Plan. Use them.

Agile: Planning is iterative — done just-in-time for each sprint during Sprint Planning.

✅ BEFORE ACTING: Call emergency planning session → develop plan → then act.
10 Consult the team before making decisions — they have the expertise Decision ▾

The PM facilitates decisions; the team makes them. Never make technical decisions alone — especially architectural or domain-specific ones.

⚠ TRAP: "Make the decision yourself based on your research and inform the team" = PM acting alone = wrong.
11 Choose actions that best serve project objectives & deliver highest value to stakeholders Decision ▾

We do projects for customers. When forced to prioritize: project security/integrity > customer satisfaction > internal team comfort > cosmetic/CEO requests.

✅ PRIORITY ORDER (when choosing): Project health → Customer value → Team → Individual preference.
⚠ TRAP: "Split the team's time to do all three" — if the question says you can only do one, don't go against the constraint.
12 Understand root cause of conflict BEFORE resolving it Decision ▾

Never solve a problem you don't fully understand. Meet one-on-one with the parties first → understand underlying cause → then facilitate resolution.

✅ SEQUENCE: One-on-ones to understand → THEN facilitated discussion to resolve.
⚠ TRAP: Jumping straight to "implement a new process" without understanding the cause = wrong.
13 When an issue occurs: log it in the Issue Log → check Risk Register for pre-planned response Decision ▾

Risk = uncertain event (hasn't happened). Issue = event that HAS happened and is negatively affecting the project.

When an issue occurs → add to Issue Log → look in Risk Register for the response you planned in advance.

⚠ TRAP: "Add to risk register" when something is already happening = wrong (it's now an issue, not a risk).
✅ FIRST STEP when issue occurs: Add to Issue Log → assign owner → check Risk Register for response.
14 When confused, consult Subject Matter Experts, Lessons Learned, or OPAs Decision ▾

PMs are not subject matter experts. For technical, legal, compliance questions — find the right expert. Check lessons learned from past projects. Use organizational process assets (OPAs).

⚠ TRAP: "Make the best decision you can and adjust later" when expertise is needed = wrong. Escalating to sponsor for expert knowledge = wrong (sponsor is not the SME).
15 Always investigate & gather information BEFORE acting — especially when asked "what to do first" Decision ▾

Never fix an issue without first analyzing it. Review the deliverable, check documentation, gather facts before picking up the phone or acting.

Stakeholder management (angry client, team conflict) = your jurisdiction as PM. Don't escalate to sponsor for these.

⚠ TRAP: "Call the client and apologize immediately" before reviewing = acting without analyzing = wrong.
16 Show progress through tangible outputs — MVPs and prototypes Decision ▾

MVP (Minimum Viable Product) = deployable, real value, customers can use and earn from. Prototype = demo/feel, no production value yet.

When stakeholders express concern about progress → show them working functionality, not status reports or verbal updates.

✅ KEY (Agile): MVPs are a hot exam topic. Best way to get customer feedback and early value delivery.
17 Resolve issues at your level — only escalate for approvals beyond your authority Decision ▾

Team conflict, morale, vendor problems, stakeholder issues = YOUR job. Don't ask your sponsor or senior management to solve day-to-day project problems.

Escalate ONLY when: you need formal approval, a decision exceeds your authority limit, or a legal/compliance issue requires executive action.

⚠ TRAP: "Ask sponsor to step in and clarify roles" = giving away your PM responsibility = wrong.
🤝 Team Leadership & Collaboration — Principles 18–27
18 Be a Servant Leader — support, empower, and shield the team. Don't dictate. Team ▾

Servant leadership = "fetch food and water." Give the team what they need to succeed. Listen, coach, encourage. Remove obstacles. Value their opinions.

The PM facilitates; the team solves problems. Never select the solution for the team — let them choose.

⚠ TRAP: "Research the solution yourself and direct the team" = dictator behavior = wrong.
19 Act as an integrator — look at ALL components of the project, not just one Team ▾

A project has multiple work streams. Your role is to identify dependencies, resolve conflicts, and align timelines across ALL of them — not just the one that's behind.

⚠ TRAP: "Focus on the area farthest behind" = ignoring integration = wrong. "Delegate to each team to resolve conflicts" = abdicating integrator role = wrong.
20 The team is best suited to break down work (WBS, user stories) Team ▾

The PM should not create the WBS alone. The team — as subject matter experts — knows how the work breaks down. Wait for them or build a framework and have them detail it.

✅ BEST: Wait for the team to create WBS together, even if it causes a short delay.
21 The team determines activity timing and duration estimates Team ▾

Ask the team to determine sequencing and time estimates. Don't build the schedule yourself and present it to them.

✅ KEY: Share requirements → let team determine sequence and durations. Their estimates = most accurate.
22 Understand team motivations — a demotivated team kills the project Team ▾

Consistently motivate the team. When a high performer seems disengaged: meet privately, understand why, before taking any performance action.

Review: Maslow's Hierarchy, McGregor's Theory X/Y, Herzberg's Two-Factor Theory for exam questions.

⚠ TRAP: "Reassign to less critical tasks" or "implement performance measures immediately" before meeting with the person = wrong.
23 Always be ethical — ethics trumps everything except stopping a project Team ▾

Don't lie, cheat, steal, or break the law. If the project is breaking the law → stop it. Ethics > company pressure > project continuity.

If a team member is committing fraud (time sheet inflation, etc.) → address it directly with them and document. Don't overlook it for "project needs."

⚠ TRAP: "Overlook it since they're technically competent" = ethical violation = wrong.
24 Create a safe environment for disagreements — conflict can be constructive Team ▾

Conflict = people care. Disengaged stakeholders who say "fine, whatever" are more dangerous than vocal ones. Encourage open disagreement.

Your job: keep conflict at a manageable level (L1-L2), not eliminate it. Set ground rules that welcome questions and alternative views.

⚠ TRAP: Meeting people privately and presenting concerns anonymously = hiding conflict, not creating safe environment = suboptimal.
25 Protect the team's focus — avoid task overload and unsustainable overtime Team ▾

Overtime is unsustainable. Task overload leads to poor quality. Don't assign additional work to a person already at full capacity without consulting them.

✅ KEY: Discuss with the person → let them assess their own capacity → decline if it compromises primary responsibilities.
26 Use peer programming and peer learning for skill development Team ▾

When team lacks certain skills, don't immediately hire externals or enroll everyone in formal training. Let experts within the team teach others through peer sessions.

27 The "Be Nice" Elimination Principle — use to eliminate bad choices Team ▾

Use this to immediately eliminate answer choices:

  • ❌ Don't fire anyone (unless ethics forces it)
  • ❌ Don't force overtime
  • ❌ Don't call out individuals in front of the group
  • ❌ Don't punish before meeting with the person first
  • ❌ Don't tell people to "wait" or push them off
  • ❌ Don't escalate to HR / sponsor before attempting resolution yourself
✅ RULE: Always meet with the person privately first → understand → then act.
⚡ Agile-Specific Principles — Principles 28–31
28 Only the Product Owner documents and prioritizes the product backlog Agile ▾
Agile

PM cannot add to, remove from, or prioritize the product backlog. Team cannot write user stories. Only the Product Owner does this.

Keywords signaling Agile: backlog, product owner, sprint, iteration, scrum, scrum master, Kanban.

⚠ TRAP: "Let the team start working on features they identified as high priority" = team doing PO's job = wrong.
29 Agile teams prefer collocation, face-to-face communication, and visual workflows Agile ▾
Agile

Collocation = team working in the same physical space. Most effective for collaboration. If team is in the same city → arrange collocated work.

Information radiators = burndown charts, Kanban/Scrum boards visible to all. Low-tech, high-touch. Face-to-face with whiteboard = most effective communication.

⚠ TRAP: If team is in same city, "invest in better video tools" < "arrange collocated work." If teams are global, video tools may be valid.
30 Roll out methodology changes gradually — pilot first, then scale Agile ▾

Transitioning from Waterfall to Agile? Don't big-bang it. Select 1–2 pilot teams, gather lessons learned, refine the approach, then roll out to others.

⚠ TRAP: "Implement agile across all teams simultaneously right after training" = chaos = wrong.
31 Agile requires ongoing customer feedback — customer must be consistently engaged Agile ▾
Agile

Customer must attend Sprint Review meetings. If they become hard to reach → adjust the sprint schedule to accommodate them. Do NOT continue building without their input.

⚠ TRAP: "Have Product Owner make validation decisions on behalf of customer" = cutting customer out = wrong.
✅ KEY: Adjust schedule to get feedback > pause development completely.
✅ Quality & Delivery — Principles 32–34
32 Define quality requirements EARLY — quality is subjective and customer-specific Quality ▾
Traditional: Quality Mgmt PlanAgile: Definition of Done

Scope requirements = what it should do. Quality requirements = what will make the customer unhappy if wrong (performance, reliability, aesthetics).

Define these with stakeholders BEFORE development begins. "We'll figure it out as we go" = unacceptable.

⚠ TRAP: "Use industry-standard practices and proceed" = assuming quality without confirming = wrong.
33 Customers must validate deliverables for scope and quality — don't skip validation Quality ▾
Traditional: Validate Scope + Control QualityAgile: Sprint Review

Get customer (or a proxy) to validate before moving to next phase. Don't deploy based purely on internal technical checks.

✅ KEY: If customer is unavailable → use a proxy for preliminary validation rather than delaying the whole phase.
34 Use inclusive, low-tech collaboration tools — whiteboards over complex software Quality ▾
Agile preferred

When collaborating across mixed technical backgrounds — use whiteboards, sketches, and in-person workshops. Don't use advanced modeling software that non-technical stakeholders can't follow.

Never silo work. Agile is collaborative by nature.

✅ PRINCIPLE: Low tech, high touch = best for cross-functional collaboration.
📐 Estimation — Principle 35
35 Use bottom-up estimating for most accurate results (Traditional) / Story points & planning poker (Agile) Quality ▾
Bottom-up = detailed, accurateAgile = story points, Fibonacci, velocity

Bottom-up: Break work into smallest tasks → estimate each → aggregate. Slow but most accurate. Team members who will do the work provide the estimates.

Agile velocity: Backlog total points ÷ points per sprint = number of iterations = project timeline.

⚠ TRAP: "Use historical data from similar projects" = top-down/analogous = less accurate than bottom-up.
⚠️ Risk Management — Principles 36–38
36 Identify and document risks early AND continuously throughout the project Risk ▾

Positive risks = opportunities (can reduce schedule/cost). Negative risks = threats (delays, overruns).

Risk identification is NOT a one-time activity. Risk evolves continuously. New risks can appear; old ones can disappear. Risk Register = living document.

⚠ TRAP: "Begin migration work immediately" before risk assessment = wrong. Never start significant work without identifying risks.
37 Document responses for BOTH threats (negative) and opportunities (positive) in the Risk Register Risk ▾

Risk Register = stores the risks AND their responses (both positive and negative). Risk Management Plan = tells you HOW to manage risk — does NOT contain actual risks.

⚠ TRAP: "Update the Risk Management Plan when a new risk is found" = wrong. Update the Risk Register.
✅ SEQUENCE for positive risk (opportunity): Document in Risk Register first → develop response → then discuss with stakeholders.
38 Vendor/procurement: use mutually beneficial contracts — no lopsided deals Risk ▾

Contract types (when to use):

  • Time & Material: Scope is high-level/unclear
  • Cost Reimbursable: Share risk between buyer and vendor
  • Fixed Price: Scope is well-defined, you want no risk

Negotiate fair contracts. Steep penalties + unlimited liability + limited payment = lopsided = ethical problem.

⚠ TRAP: "Proceed with contract that maximizes protection for your organization" = ignoring vendor's interests = wrong.
🔁 Lifecycle & Closure — Principles 39–40
39 Update the Lessons Learned Register THROUGHOUT the project — not just at the end Lifecycle ▾
Traditional: Manage Project KnowledgeAgile: Retrospectives

Learn something now → log it now. Don't wait for the end of phase or project. Current and future projects benefit from real-time lessons.

⚠ TRAP: "Wait until the project retrospective at the end to document all lessons" = too late, loses current-project benefit = wrong.
40 ALL projects must be formally closed — even if terminated early Lifecycle ▾

Even if the CEO cancels the project mid-way → you must formally close it. This includes: paying outstanding bills, releasing team members, documenting what was and wasn't done.

⚠ TRAP: "Immediately transition the team to the new project as requested" = skipping formal closure = wrong.
✅ CLOSURE CHECKLIST: Pay all bills → release resources → document project status → formal project closure report.
🚀 Continuous Improvement — Principles 41–44
41 Repeat and reinforce the project vision to the team throughout execution Improve ▾

Team members get buried in their work and forget the bigger picture. Regularly remind them of the project's purpose and desired outcomes.

⚠ TRAP: "Continue letting the team focus on technical excellence since they're making good progress" = doing nothing = wrong.
42 Clarify what success AND failure look like for all stakeholders Improve ▾

Different stakeholders may define success differently. Align them early. Use techniques like "remember the future" (agile) — ask: what does a successful deployment look like? What would cause this to fail?

✅ KEY: Work with ALL stakeholders to define specific, measurable success/failure criteria together.
43 Agile retrospectives are mandatory — even when the sprint went well Improve ▾
Agile: End of every sprint

Retrospective = held at end of every sprint. Review: what worked, what didn't, what to do more/less of. Even successful sprints can be improved.

⚠ TRAP: "Skip the retrospective since everything went well" = never acceptable = wrong.
44 Implement feedback loops — apply lessons from one task immediately to the next Improve ▾

When you learn something from module 1, apply it to module 2 BEFORE you start module 2. Don't document lessons "for future projects" when you can use them right now.

⚠ TRAP: "Continue with the current approach since the team gained experience" = not applying the lesson = wrong.
💰 Cost & Schedule Management — Principles 45–46
45 If forced to choose: fix budget overruns before schedule overruns (Traditional) / Adjust scope to protect budget+schedule (Agile) Lifecycle ▾
Traditional: budget firstAgile: adjust scope, fixed time+cost

Traditional: Scope fixed, time/cost adjusted. When choosing, prioritize budget correction then schedule compression.

Agile: Time and budget are fixed (time-boxed sprints, fixed team). Scope adjusts to fit. Product Owner deprioritizes backlog items as needed.

46 Critical Path determines project duration — compress it to shorten the schedule Lifecycle ▾
Traditional

Critical Path = longest path through the network diagram = project duration. Activities on critical path have zero float.

Schedule delays → identify which delays are ON the critical path. Only those actually affect the end date.

✅ EXAM TRAP: "The security review slipped 3 days" — only matters if security review is on the critical path.
🎯 Exam Strategy Principles — Principles 47–50
47 Eliminate answers with absolute language — "always," "all," "never" (as a wrong answer) are red flags Strategy ▾

Project management is situational. Answers with "always do X" or "all stakeholders must..." are usually wrong. Exception: ethical principles where "never" is correct (never break the law, etc.).

✅ QUICK SCAN: If an answer says "always" or "all" for a process or communication rule, eliminate it first.
48 Never do nothing — never select the "continue as-is / do nothing / wait" option Strategy ▾

If an answer says "continue doing what you're doing" or "wait until it becomes a bigger problem" — eliminate it. Good PMs are proactive, not passive.

⚠ TRAP: "Continue current development; revisit if API issues worsen" = reactive, passive = wrong. Always take some proactive action.
49 The perfect answer is not always listed — choose the BEST of the four, not the perfect one Strategy ▾

Sometimes all four choices have flaws. Your job is not to find the perfect answer — it's to eliminate the three worse answers and pick the least bad one.

Use process of elimination aggressively. Apply mindset principles to rule out choices, then choose what's left.

✅ METHOD: Eliminate clearly wrong (fire, overtime, sponsor for team issues, act without plan) → pick best of remaining.
50 Mindset principles are guidelines, not absolute rules — context can override them Strategy ▾

Example: "Never fire a team member" is usually right — but if they're stealing company data or breaking the law, you may have to. Ethics > every other principle.

When all 4 choices violate some principle, choose the one that violates the fewest or least important ones. Objective: pass the test by selecting the best of the four.

✅ FINAL RULE: Ethics > Project Objectives > Stakeholder value > Team > Individual. When in doubt, pick what's best for the project and the customer.
